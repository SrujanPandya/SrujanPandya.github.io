The Architecture of Solitude in Modern Cities

In our hyperconnected age, urban spaces paradoxically offer new forms of isolation. How contemporary architecture shapes our experience of being alone together.

By Jane Writer · December 1, 2024 · 12 min read

There is a particular quality to being alone in a city of millions. Not loneliness exactly, but something more ambiguous—a solitude that is paradoxically enabled by proximity to others. In Tokyo's capsule hotels, in the anonymous corridors of modern office buildings, in the crowd-filled yet isolating spaces of shopping malls, contemporary urban architecture has created new forms of being alone together.

The architectural historian Beatriz Colomina has argued that modern architecture has always been concerned with privacy, with creating spaces for the individual to retreat from society. But what happens when those spaces for retreat become the dominant mode of urban existence? When being alone becomes not the exception but the default condition?

The Capsule Hotel as Metaphor

Consider the Japanese capsule hotel, that most extreme example of spatial efficiency and social atomization. Designed in the 1970s by architect Kisho Kurokawa, these hotels reduce the individual sleeping space to its absolute minimum—a tube barely large enough for a human body, stacked like wine bottles in a cellar.

Yet the capsule hotel is not simply about saving space. It represents a particular vision of urban life: one where individuals circulate through the city like blood cells through arteries, identical and interchangeable, requiring only the most minimal infrastructure for rest before returning to the flow. The capsule is architecture stripped of all domesticity, of all the accumulations and decorations that make a space personal. It is shelter reduced to its pure function.

What's remarkable is not that such spaces exist—hotels have always been transient, impersonal places—but that they seem to represent an ideal of contemporary urban living. Micro-apartments, co-living spaces, and "wellness pods" proliferate in expensive cities worldwide, each promising the same combination of minimal private space within dense urban contexts.

Glass Walls and Digital Screens

The modern office building presents another form of solitary togetherness. Floor-to-ceiling glass walls promise transparency and openness, yet create a fishbowl effect where everyone can see everyone else but meaningful interaction is discouraged. Open-plan offices were supposed to foster collaboration, but noise-canceling headphones have become essential equipment, creating bubbles of isolation within the supposed communality.

Meanwhile, the proliferation of screens—in our pockets, on our desks, mounted on every wall—creates a strange doubling of presence. We sit next to our colleagues while communicating primarily through Slack messages. We ride the subway surrounded by other bodies, each absorbed in their own glowing rectangle of private experience.

The Vanishing Commons

Perhaps what distinguishes contemporary urban solitude from earlier forms is the disappearance of genuine public space—what the sociologist Ray Oldenburg called "third places," spaces that are neither home nor work but something in between. Coffee shops have become de facto offices, libraries transform into study halls, and public squares are designed primarily for circulation rather than congregation.

The pandemic only accelerated this trend. Now even our domestic spaces have been colonized by work, fitness classes conducted via Zoom in our living rooms, happy hours held in our kitchens. The boundaries between different modes of existence have collapsed into a single continuous space of productivity and surveillance.

Finding Refuge

And yet, there remains something potentially valuable in this urban solitude. The philosopher Hannah Arendt distinguished between loneliness—the painful feeling of being cut off from others—and solitude, which she saw as a necessary condition for thought. "In solitude," she wrote, "I am 'by myself,' together with my self, and therefore two-in-one, whereas in loneliness I am actually one, abandoned by all others."

The question is whether the forms of solitude offered by contemporary architecture allow for this kind of productive being-with-oneself, or whether they simply produce a different form of loneliness—one that is harder to recognize precisely because it is so ordinary, so built into the structures we inhabit.

Walking through any major city today, one sees countless people alone—eating alone in restaurants, working alone in cafes, living alone in tiny apartments. Is this freedom or isolation? The answer, probably, is both. What matters is whether we have chosen this solitude or whether it has simply been chosen for us by the economic and architectural forces that shape contemporary urban life.

Perhaps the task is not to reject urban solitude entirely but to reclaim it—to find ways of being alone that are genuinely restorative rather than merely isolating. This might mean defending the remaining public spaces where solitude can coexist with sociability. It might mean resisting the colonization of every moment by productivity. It might simply mean learning to distinguish between loneliness and solitude, between isolation and the necessary retreat into oneself.

The architecture of our cities will continue to shape these possibilities. The question is whether we can shape it in turn.
